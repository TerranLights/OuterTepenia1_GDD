# Nerve — Expanded Systems (TENTATIVE)

**Status**: Draft for design review. Everything here is a proposal. Keep, modify, or discard any element during a dedicated design pass.

**Goal**: Raise Nerve from 9/44 skills (20%) to a stat that is as socially present as Humanity, as tactically present as Investigation, and uniquely responsible for the *psychological layer* of the game — willpower, authority, morale, and the ability to hold together under pressure.

**Core distinction from Humanity**:
- **Humanity** = emotional warmth, empathy, connection, being *liked*
- **Nerve** = psychological force, conviction, composure, being *respected or feared*

A high-Humanity / low-Nerve character is warm and beloved but can be pushed around. A high-Nerve / low-Humanity character commands rooms but struggles to form genuine bonds. Both extremes have tradeoffs. The most effective social builds invest in both.

---

## System 1: Psychological Intimidation in Dialogue

Distinct from Might's physical intimidation. Nerve-based intimidation is about *authority* — the calm, composed certainty that you will not yield, and that consequences will follow if the NPC doesn't comply.

**Proposed threshold dialogue unlocks:**

| Nerve | Unlocks |
|-------|---------|
| 4     | Can hold a firm position without being argued out of it; NPCs register you as determined |
| 5     | Basic psychological pressure: can make NPCs uncomfortable enough to reconsider |
| 7     | Can break the composure of most non-hardened NPCs; authority is palpable |
| 8     | Can intimidate NPCs who would not yield to physical threats — faction leaders, council members, ideologues |
| 9     | Even deeply committed characters may hesitate; willpower becomes a weapon in its own right |
| 10    | Can hold a room of hostile NPCs in check through presence alone (situational, not universal) |

**Design notes:**
- Nerve intimidation works best against NPCs who *think* — politicians, faction leaders, ideologues, commanders. It fails against NPCs who have nothing to lose or who cannot be reasoned with.
- Might intimidation works better against *fighters*. Nerve intimidation works better against *thinkers*.
- Combined high Might + high Nerve: the rarest and most complete authority. Physical and psychological simultaneously.
- Nerve intimidation should feel *controlled and quiet*, not aggressive. The character that speaks slowly and doesn't blink is more unnerving than the one that shouts.

## System 2: Coercion Resistance

When NPCs or factions attempt to manipulate, pressure, threaten, or bribe the player character, Nerve determines how much agency the player retains in the outcome.

**Proposed system:**

Low Nerve (1–4):
- Certain dialogue branches close — the player character is visibly rattled, which removes options
- Blackmail threats may force concessions in dialogue (losing resources, reputation, or information)
- Faction pressure (e.g. "join us or we cut off your siligel supply") may lock the player into compliance without a resistance option
- Bribery pressure: the player character may have to accept worse trade terms without pushback

High Nerve (7–10):
- All coercion attempts unlock a resistance dialogue option
- Nerve 8+: can turn a coercion attempt back on the NPC ("Now we know what you're afraid of")
- Nerve 10: cannot be blackmailed; NPCs recognize it and generally don't try

**Design notes:**
- This system does *not* make low-Nerve characters helpless — it shifts the cost. A low-Nerve character still has options, but some of those options require paying a price (resources, reputation, or a favor) that a high-Nerve character can decline.
- This creates a meaningful distinction: Humanity avoids making enemies. Nerve resists the enemies you make.
- Pairs well with Investigation (recognizing the manipulation early) + Nerve (resisting it).

## System 3: Enemy Morale and Panic

When combat goes badly for the enemy, Nerve affects how much the player character's presence contributes to morale collapse. This makes high-Nerve builds *psychologically lethal* in combat without increasing raw damage output.

**Proposed triggers for morale checks:**
- When an enemy in a group takes a critical hit or is killed
- When the enemy group has lost 50% of its members
- When the player activates a specific high-Nerve combat behavior (TBD)
- When an enemy is forced into a clearly losing position (surrounded, disarmed, low health)

**Proposed Nerve modifier to morale checks:**

| Nerve | Morale Effect |
|-------|---------------|
| 1–3   | No morale effect; enemies fight to the end normally |
| 4–6   | Minor: enemies at critical HP may attempt to flee |
| 7–8   | Moderate: enemy groups may break and scatter after heavy losses |
| 9     | Strong: individual enemies may surrender mid-combat |
| 10    | Some enemies won't engage at all once they understand who they're fighting |

**Enemy morale outcomes (tentative options per encounter):**
- **Flee**: enemy abandons combat and runs; may leave equipment behind
- **Surrender**: enemy drops weapon and raises hands; becomes a dialogue/negotiation target
- **Hesitate**: enemy skips their next turn (frozen in fear); no loyalty to fight on
- **Hold**: enemy overcomes the morale check and continues fighting (common for zealots, robots, desperate NPCs)

**Design notes:**
- Enemy type affects base morale resistance. Gang members break easily; fanatical faction soldiers may never break regardless of Nerve; robots with no self-preservation drive are immune unless specifically scripted.
- This rewards high-Nerve players with *shorter, less damaging* combat encounters — not by dealing more damage, but by ending fights faster through psychological leverage.
- Companion Nerve checks (see System 4) and enemy morale checks use the same underlying system — morale is a shared mechanic for both sides of combat.

## System 4: Companion Loyalty and Crisis Behavior

Nerve governs how deeply companions will follow the player character, and how they behave when things go catastrophically wrong.

**Proposed loyalty tiers:**

| Nerve | Companion Behavior |
|-------|-------------------|
| 1–2   | Companions follow basic requests only; refuse anything dangerous; may abandon if threatened |
| 3–4   | Companions will follow into moderate danger but express doubt; can be argued out of risky orders |
| 5–6   | Companions follow reasonable orders without pushback; default behavior |
| 7–8   | Companions follow into genuinely dangerous situations out of trust in the player character |
| 9     | Companions hold position even under severe pressure; hold their nerve when things collapse |
| 10    | Companions have unshakeable belief; will take actions they wouldn't take for anyone else |

**Crisis behavior** (when a companion is nearly dead or the situation becomes dire):

| Nerve | Companion Crisis Response |
|-------|--------------------------|
| 1–4   | Companion morale collapses; flees or freezes; requires re-engagement after combat |
| 5–6   | Companion holds but becomes defensive (stops attacking, focuses on survival) |
| 7–9   | Companion maintains combat effectiveness; may push harder |
| 10    | Companion enters a composed last-stand state; no panic at any health level |

**Design notes:**
- Humanity determines *how much companions like the player*. Nerve determines *how far they will go* based on that relationship. Both matter. A companion who loves a low-Nerve character will still hesitate at the brink.
- Some companions (personality-dependent) may respond more to Humanity than Nerve, and vice versa. Personality design for each companion should specify which threshold system is primary.
- This system also creates interesting play with the Isolation & Psychological Resilience skill (Nerve + Engine) — that skill already represents the player character's own resilience; the companion system mirrors it outward.

## System 5: Sustained Pressure in Long Dialogues

In extended dialogue sequences — formal negotiations, council debates, prolonged interrogations, faction summits — Nerve determines whether the player character can maintain their position over time.

**Proposed system:**

Some dialogue sequences are multi-stage encounters (similar to how combat is multi-turn). Each stage, the opposing NPC or faction applies pressure. The player must either make concessions or pass Nerve checks to hold their ground.

**Without high Nerve:**
- Long negotiations accumulate pressure; eventually the player must give something to continue
- In council debates, low Nerve means the player character's position is progressively undermined unless they have overwhelming Humanity or faction support
- Interrogation targets can outlast a low-Nerve player character

**With high Nerve (7+):**
- Player can sustain their position for all stages without forced concessions
- Nerve 8+: Can actually tire the opposing party — NPCs become *less* effective as the dialogue continues, eventually conceding themselves
- Nerve 10: No prolonged dialogue encounter can force a concession

**Design notes:**
- This system makes Nerve genuinely valuable for players who want to engage deeply with political and faction content, not just combat.
- Pairs with Diplomatic Negotiation skill (H+N) — the skill handles the specific technique; Nerve handles the stamina to maintain it.
- Not every dialogue needs this system — only high-stakes, multi-stage scenes. Regular conversations resolve normally.

## System 6: Willpower and Psychological Debuff Resistance

Nerve already provides resistance to fear, terror, and morale effects (documented in the Influence Map). Expanded specifics:

**Proposed debuff categories Nerve resists:**

| Debuff Type | Low Nerve Effect | High Nerve (7+) Effect |
|------------|-----------------|----------------------|
| Terror / Fear | –2 AP next turn, flee impulse | Full immunity at Nerve 9+ |
| Intimidation (from NPCs) | Forced dialogue concession | Resistance option always available |
| Confusion (Arcanet interference, blackouts) | –1 AP, reduced accuracy | Halved duration; Nerve 10 immune |
| Morale collapse (party wipe risk) | AP penalty, accuracy drop | No penalty; continues fighting at full effectiveness |
| Psychological attacks (certain robot enemies) | Temporary stat reduction | Reduced duration and magnitude |
| Prolonged isolation debuff | Stacks quickly, harder to clear | Stacks slowly; Isolation & Resilience skill reduces further |

**Design notes:**
- Concordia City's Scorpio District is specifically characterized by psychological toll and despair. High-Nerve characters will have meaningfully different experiences in that district — quests that other characters find destabilizing, they move through with composure.
- Arcanet blackouts and "voices in the static" (Reason 6 in the grid document) specifically affect low-Nerve characters more severely.

## Tentative New Skills (Optional — for design consideration)

Not committed additions. Options if Nerve still feels thin after the above systems.

**Option A: Psychological Warfare & Morale Manipulation** (Nerve + Investigation)  
Active use of psychological knowledge in combat — recognizing enemy morale states, exploiting hesitation, using environmental cues to accelerate panic. Makes Nerve a combat stat through intelligence rather than raw damage.

**Option B: Sustained Interrogation & Information Extraction** (Nerve + Humanity)  
Specialized skill for extracting information through prolonged dialogue pressure. Bridges Nerve (endurance) with Humanity (reading the subject). Could unlock quest paths that pure Investigation builds can't access.

**Option C: Command Protocols** (Nerve)  
A pure-Nerve skill for directing combat — giving companions orders mid-combat, coordinating group positioning, suppressing enemy morale. Would make Nerve a tactical leadership stat even in encounters where the player isn't in direct combat.

## Summary: What Nerve Would Now Govern

| Domain | System |
|--------|--------|
| Social leverage | Psychological intimidation in dialogue |
| Social defense | Coercion resistance (blackmail, threats, pressure) |
| Combat utility | Enemy morale and panic (shortening fights) |
| Companion depth | Loyalty ceiling and crisis behavior |
| Political / faction content | Sustained pressure in long dialogues |
| Passive resilience | Psychological debuff resistance (fear, confusion, isolation) |
| AP formula | `floor((Nerve – 5) ÷ 2)` modifier per turn |
| NODE system | Focus bonus to hit chance; Focus Drain resistance |
| Skill coverage | 10 existing skills (Diplomatic Negotiation, Faction Management, Faction Rhetoric, Deception, Moral Philosophy, Companion Command, Isolation & Resilience, Defensive Posturing, Threat Assessment, Ossuary Resonance) |
